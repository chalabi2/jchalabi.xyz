# Drone Detection at an Air Force Base

I had the opportunity to work on something I didn't expect to find myself doing, real-time drone detection in restricted airspace, alongside the ******* Police Department and **** Air Force Base.

## The Problem

Air shows generate a lot of enthusiasm, and with that comes a lot of people flying consumer drones in places they absolutely shouldn't be. **** AFB operates under strict airspace restrictions, and during an air show those restrictions extend to a 5-mile no-fly zone around the base. Despite that, drones were entering the zone during the event and neither local law enforcement nor base personnel had a reliable way to detect them in real time, let alone locate their operators on the ground.

The obvious instinct is to reach for radar. It's the gold standard for airspace monitoring, and most people, including myself, assume it's already in place everywhere. What I learned is that for a temporary deployment in a public environment, radar isn't as straightforward as it sounds. Getting FCC approval to actively transmit in a crowded public space turned out to be a slow, bureaucratic process that wasn't going to move at the pace the situation required. That gap is what created the opportunity to try a different approach.

## The Approach

The solution I worked on centered around a passive RF sensor, a receiver that listens for the radio frequency signals consumer drones use to communicate with their controllers, without transmitting anything itself. Because it's purely passive, it sidesteps the FCC authorization problem entirely.

On top of the sensor hardware, I built software to process and filter the incoming detection data. Raw RF data from a busy environment like an air show is noisy, there are a lot of signals flying around that aren't drones, and even legitimate drone detections need to be triaged by proximity, signal strength, and position relative to the restricted zone. The software filtered those detections down to actionable alerts and displayed confirmed drone positions on a map interface that officers could use in the field.

When a drone was flagged inside the no-fly zone, officers could pull up the map, see where the signal was coming from, and navigate directly to the operator on the ground. In practice, most consumer drones broadcast enough metadata in their RF signal, including Remote ID where supported, that you can narrow down the operator's location to within a reasonably small area, enough for a responding officer to work with.

## What Surprised Me

The part that genuinely caught me off guard was how early-stage this problem still is at the local law enforcement level. I went in assuming that departments near major military installations, particularly one as prominent as **** AFB, would already have some form of drone detection tooling in place. They don't, at least not in any standardized way.

The drone threat isn't hypothetical, it's already showing up at events, near critical infrastructure, and in airspace where manned aircraft operate. The technology to address it exists and isn't particularly exotic. But the procurement pipeline, regulatory friction, and institutional inertia mean that a lot of departments are still figuring out what to do when a drone shows up where it shouldn't be.

What makes that gap stranger is that a regulatory framework already exists for exactly this problem. The FAA's Remote ID rule has been in effect since 2023, requiring most commercially sold drones to broadcast identifying information in real time. In theory that gives law enforcement a standardized, passive data stream to work with. In practice, most departments have no tooling to receive or act on it.

## Remote ID

Remote ID is the FAA's broadcast identification standard for drones, roughly analogous to an ADS-B transponder for manned aircraft. Compliant drones continuously broadcast their serial number, GPS position, altitude, velocity, and the operator's takeoff location over a short-range radio link.

An RF sensor positioned at an event can pick up these broadcasts passively, no spectrum license required, and use them to build a real-time picture of every compliant drone in the area along with a fixed reference point for the operator. The limitation is enforcement: Remote ID only works on drones that follow the rules. A bad actor flying a modified or non-compliant drone won't show up in the broadcast feed, which is why pairing it with broader RF fingerprinting matters.

## Takeaway

This was a small engagement in the context of the broader counter-drone space, but it was a useful window into a real operational gap. The combination of passive RF sensing and lightweight software filtering is a practical starting point, it's deployable quickly, doesn't require spectrum licensing, and gives responding officers something concrete to act on.

I'm still early to this space, but it's one I'm keeping an eye on. Consumer drones are getting cheaper and more capable every year, and the tools to manage that in sensitive environments are still catching up.
